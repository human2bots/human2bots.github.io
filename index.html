Abstract

Quadrupedal robots have demonstrated the ability to locomote and manipulate in complex environments. However, equipping them with versatile and scalable manipulation skills remains a significant challenge. In this work, we present a system that integrates data collection and learning from both human and robotic sources. Specifically, we introduce a teleoperation and data collection pipeline, supported by dedicated hardware, which accounts for whole-body motions and active sensing across different embodiments. To effectively leverage this data, we propose an efficient learning architecture that supports pre-training and co-training with multimodal data from diverse embodiments, including humans. Additionally, we construct the first dataset for the LocoMan robot, encompassing various household manipulation tasks, supplemented with a corresponding human dataset. Experimental results demonstrate that our data collection and training framework significantly enhances the performance and efficiency of imitation learning, enabling more versatile quadrupedal manipulation capabilities.

Teleoperation & Data Collection System

Our teleoperation system is built upon Open-Television and Bunny-VisionPro. By leveraging the head and hand tracking capabilities of the Apple Vision Pro, we enable immersive human teleoperation with visual feedback on robot embodiments such as LocoMan. This systems allows collecting data on the robot at least 4x faster than using a traditional remote control.

Apple Vision Pro is also used for collecting human videos with captured hand motions. Through the simple interface we designed, a human operator can easily collect dozens of trajectories by themselves within minutes.

Learning Framework

Our learning framework is designed to efficiently learn from the multimodal data from both human and robot sources. To this end, we employ a Transformer-based architecture where tokenization and detokenization are performed by modules with embodiment-specific weights, but the core Transformer weights are shared across all embodiments.

Different from recent works that utilize a similar modularization for large-scale pretraining, we focus on leveraging data from a few other embodiments to improve the learning performance in the target domain. This allows us to perform modality alignment on a finer granularity and use separate tokenizers/detokenizers for each modality from one embodiment.
